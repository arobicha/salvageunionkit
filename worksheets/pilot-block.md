### What did they do before they came to the Crawler?

> _

*Mediator note: The setting splits people into corpo arco-dwellers, wasters, and salvagers. Most pilots have a *before*. An arco mechanic who deserted? A waster kid from a settlement the Crawler trades with? A corpo merc who turned coat? Push for one specific job, one specific reason for leaving.*

### How long have they been aboard the Crawler?

> _

*Mediator note: Newcomers and lifers carry different weight. A pilot aboard since childhood knows everyone's secrets; a pilot who joined six months ago is still being tested. Mix the answers across the table — homogeneous tenure flattens the politics.*

### What do they want — from the wastes, from the Crawler, from themselves?

> _

*Mediator note: This is the engine. Push past "to survive" — everyone wants that. Get to something specific. To find a missing sibling. To prove they're more than the corpo brand on their shoulder. To one day pilot a Tech 4 chassis. The *want* drives the campaign.*

### What's the one thing they don't want the rest of the crew to know?

> _

*Mediator note: Every pilot has a hidden thing. A debt to a corpo. A child in an arco. A piece of salvage they pocketed instead of sharing. Don't make it sinister by default — sometimes the secret is tender (they write poetry, they pray to a dead god, they cry in their cabin). The point is: the rest of the crew doesn't know. That's the lever.*

## Pilot — Cabin

*The Crawler is a shared home, and cabins are where pilots stop being public. The setting tells us resources are pooled and decisions are communal — but cabins are private. What's inside tells you who someone is when no one's watching.*

### Where on the Crawler is their cabin, and what's that location like?

> _

*Mediator note: Salvager Crawlers are jury-rigged. Cabins are reclaimed cargo holds, repurposed escape pods, hammocks slung in a maintenance corridor. Location implies status and personality. The medic near the infirmary. The newcomer in a damp lower deck. The old pilot in a cabin nobody else wants because the engine vibrates the wall.*

### What's the first thing you see when you step inside?

> _

*Mediator note: One concrete object. A wall of pinned scrap-art. A working pre-collapse radio. A pet (a wasteland animal, a tame creature). A clean workbench that nobody else is allowed to touch. The object characterizes them faster than a paragraph of description.*

### What keepsake do they have from before the Crawler?

> _

*Mediator note: Not everyone has one — some pilots arrived with nothing, and that *is* the answer. For those who do: a child's drawing, an arco employee badge, a busted weapon, a photograph that shouldn't have survived the radstorms. The keepsake is a hook NPCs and antagonists can pull on.*

### What's the one thing in their cabin that would surprise the rest of the crew?

> _

*Mediator note: This connects to the secret from the Profile section, but doesn't have to be the same thing. The secret is psychological; this is physical. Sometimes they overlap (the photo of the sibling they said was dead). Sometimes they don't (they keep a journal nobody knows about). Either is good.*

## Pilot — Tie to the World

*Each pilot has at least one named connection to someone in the world the table built during Session Zero. Pull from the Arcos, raider groups, settlements, and named contacts captured there. If Session Zero hasn't happened yet, invent a person and pin the region down later.*

### Who is the NPC, and where in the wastes are they?

> _

*Mediator note: Pull from Session Zero answers where possible. A named raider chief, the elder of a settlement, a corpo handler the Crawler has dealt with, a sibling who stayed behind in an Arco. The closer the NPC is to existing campaign material, the more leverage you have over them in play.*

### What's the nature of the tie — family, debt, love, hatred, rivalry, something else?

> _

*Mediator note: This NPC is now a recurring presence. They will show up — in person, by message, in someone else's mouth. Capture them clearly enough that you can write them into a future scene. "Something else" includes things like: former student, the person who saved their life, a child they fostered briefly, an ex-lover who married someone in a rival faction.*
